Bible Scripture Text (English + Original Languages)

  • Live from the network — not written into the code or baked into the model. All text (English and originals) is fetched on-demand from the public Free Use Bible API at https://bible.helloao.org.
  • When you mention a reference (e.g. "Galatians 6", "John 1:1-5", or ask about a Greek/Hebrew word), the server detects it and fetches the exact verses.
  • English grounding: User-selectable default (LSV as NASB-style literal by default; also BSB, ASV, YLT, WEB etc. via Voice Settings). All are formal/literal.
  • Greek New Testament sources (for NT passages and word studies): SBL Greek New Testament (primary modern critical text), Byzantine Greek NT, Majority Text, Textus Receptus, and others (Tischendorf, Family 35, etc.). The server automatically pulls the Greek alongside English when relevant.
  • Hebrew Old Testament sources (for OT / Tanakh): Westminster Leningrad Codex (WLC / hbo_wlc) — the standard scholarly Masoretic Text (Hebrew) used as the base for most modern OT translations. Also available in some modern Hebrew editions.
  • After the AI replies, the server scans the reply for any scripture the model referenced and fetches accurate live text (English + original where available) for the sources panel.
  • The model is instructed to quote or reference the original Greek/Hebrew text (with transliteration when helpful) when discussing specific words, grammar, or key terms, and to cite the exact source (e.g. "SBL Greek New Testament" or "Westminster Leningrad Codex").

What "Context" Means in This App

"Context" is the disciplined practice of letting The Word interpret The Word — examining a passage within its proper literary, grammatical, and canonical setting rather than in isolation. It includes:

  • Literary context: The immediate sentence, paragraph, chapter, and book — how the words function in the author's flow of argument or story.
  • Grammatical & syntactic context: In the original Greek or Hebrew, verb tense, voice, mood, word order, case, and sentence structure often carry nuances that translations can only approximate.
  • Lexical context: How the same Greek or Hebrew word is used by this author elsewhere, in the same book, or in the broader literature of the period.
  • Historical & cultural context: The world of the original audience (ancient Near East for the Old Testament; 1st-century Greco-Roman world for the New Testament).
  • Canonical context: How the passage relates to the rest of Scripture and the overall redemptive story.

How to Understand Greek & Hebrew in Context (the Process)

  1. Begin with the literal text supplied by the app (SBL Greek New Testament for the NT; Westminster Leningrad Codex for the Hebrew OT).
  2. Read the surrounding verses and chapters to grasp the author's immediate purpose.
  3. Identify significant words or constructions and study their usage in the original language (the app surfaces these when you ask about a specific term).
  4. Analyze the grammar: Who is acting? What is the tense, aspect, or emphasis? How does the sentence structure shape meaning?
  5. Consider genre (narrative, poetry, epistle, prophecy, etc.) because different genres use language differently.
  6. Compare the same words, themes, or constructions elsewhere in the author's writings and the wider canon.
  7. Only after the text has spoken in its original context do we move to application or theology — always letting the particulars of the Greek or Hebrew guide us.

The system prompt given to Grok explicitly requires this contextual, original-language-first approach for every reference to The Word.

Citations You Hear When It Speaks

The system prompt requires the model to include explicit, natural-sounding citations — including original languages — in every response that discusses scripture:

“John 1:1 (SBL Greek New Testament) reads: ‘Ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος...’ (En archē ēn ho logos...)”
“The Hebrew of Genesis 1:1 (Westminster Leningrad Codex) is: ‘בְּרֵאשִׁית בָּרָא אֱלֹהִים’ (Bereshit bara Elohim...)”

Because the spoken text contains the citations, you hear the English translation source and the original Greek/Hebrew every time the voice reads the answer.
